Slide 2 — The frame
  • Set the frame. Voice is the most natural interface humans have — we learn it before we read or type.
  • The whole field of audio analytics is about teaching a computer to listen: to turn pressure waves into something it can search, classify and understand.
  • We will call this back at the close.

Slide 3 — A new modality, the same playbook
  • Bridge from Day 1. Everything so far was text, tables and numbers. Today the raw material is sound.
  • But the playbook is identical: raw data, to features, to a model. We are just swapping in a new, richer kind of raw data.
  • Frame audio as another modality that plugs into the same pipeline they already know.

Slide 4 — Voice is everywhere in the contact centre
  • Why a bank cares about voice. These are real, deployed categories across retail and contact-centre operations.
  • Transcription unlocks search and analytics on calls; voice biometrics authenticate a caller; sentiment and complaint mining surface issues; IVR routes; compliance QA checks recorded calls.
  • Each one starts with the same step: turn audio into something a model can read.

Slide 5 — Sensitive audio never leaves the bank
  • The non-negotiable constraint. Customer audio is some of the most sensitive data we hold — it cannot be shipped to a closed cloud API.
  • So we build entirely with open-source, self-hostable tools that keep audio inside OCBC infrastructure.
  • The stack we will use today: librosa and torchaudio for processing, and Whisper for speech-to-text — all open and on-prem.

Slide 6 — Capture, pre-process, feature, model
  • The spine of the whole day. Audio analytics is four steps: capture the sound, pre-process it, extract features, then model.
  • Modelling here means transcribe, classify, or verify — depending on the task.
  • Every section maps onto one of these boxes. Keep returning to this pipeline.

Slide 7 — Huge, noisy, and meaning lives over time
  • Why audio needs special treatment. It is enormous — thousands of samples every second, so even a short call is millions of numbers.
  • It is noisy, variable in length, and hugely diverse across accents and languages.
  • And crucially, the meaning lives in patterns over time, not in any single value. That is exactly why we transform it before modelling.

Slide 8 — Part 1 — From Sound Waves to Numbers
  • Part 1, about 26 minutes. The signal-processing foundations — sampling, Nyquist, and the all-important spectrogram.
  • Intuition only: Fourier is splitting a chord into notes; a spectrogram is a picture of frequencies over time.
  • This sets up the features in Part 2 and Whisper in Part 3.

Slide 9 — A pressure wave: amplitude and frequency
  • Start at the physics. Sound is a pressure wave travelling through air — air molecules pushed together and pulled apart, over time.
  • Two properties matter: amplitude, how big the swing is, which we hear as loudness; and frequency, how fast it oscillates, which we hear as pitch.
  • Everything downstream is about measuring and representing this wave.

Slide 10 — Amplitude over time — the raw signal
  • Plot amplitude against time and you get the waveform — the raw signal a microphone records.
  • This is a short spoken word: you can see the bursts of energy for each syllable and the quiet gaps between.
  • It is honest and complete, but as we will see, it is not yet something a model can easily learn from.

Slide 11 — Measure the wave many times per second
  • How does a continuous wave become digital? We sample it — measure the amplitude many times per second.
  • Each measurement is one sample, a single number. Watch the dots land on the continuous curve; the row of dots is the digital signal.
  • The wave is continuous; the computer only ever sees these discrete dots.

Slide 12 — How many samples per second?
  • Sample rate is how many samples per second, measured in hertz. Three to remember.
  • 8 kHz is telephone and call-centre audio. 16 kHz is the speech and ASR standard — exactly what Whisper expects. 44.1 kHz is CD-quality music.
  • Higher rate means more detail, but also more data to store and process. For speech, 16 kHz is the sweet spot.

Slide 13 — Sample at least twice the frequency
  • The Nyquist theorem tells you how high to set the sample rate. To capture a frequency, you must sample at least twice as fast as it.
  • So 16 kHz captures frequencies up to 8 kHz — which covers the range that makes speech intelligible.
  • It is also why telephone audio at 8 kHz, capturing only up to 4 kHz, sounds thin: the higher frequencies that give voices their crispness are simply gone.

Slide 14 — Each sample is rounded to a digital level
  • One more digitisation detail: bit depth. Sampling fixes the timing; bit depth fixes the precision of each measurement.
  • Each sample is rounded to the nearest available digital level — that rounding is quantisation. 16-bit is the standard, giving over sixty-five thousand levels.
  • More bits means finer steps and less quantisation noise. Keep it light — 16-bit is plenty for our work.

Slide 15 — A wall of numbers hides the meaning
  • Here is the problem. The waveform is complete, but to a model it is just a wall of numbers — tens of thousands per second.
  • The information we care about — which sounds, which words — is not in any single sample. It is hidden in how the frequencies change over time.
  • That motivates the next move: stop looking at amplitude over time, and start looking at frequency.

Slide 16 — Split a chord back into its notes
  • How do we get to frequencies? The Fourier transform. The key idea: any signal, however complex, can be broken down into the simple frequencies that compose it.
  • The analogy that sticks: separating a musical chord back into the individual notes that were played together.
  • The FFT — fast Fourier transform — does this efficiently. No math today, just the picture: one complex wave, in; its component frequencies, out.

Slide 17 — A picture of frequency over time
  • Now the single most important visualisation in audio. Slice the signal into short frames, run an FFT on each, and stack the results side by side.
  • You get a picture: time runs along the x-axis, frequency up the y-axis, and brightness shows energy at each frequency and moment.
  • This is the spectrogram. Watch the waveform morph into it. From here on, almost everything we do works on this picture, not the raw wave.

Slide 18 — Chop into short, overlapping frames
  • One implementation detail that matters. Speech is only roughly stable for very short stretches, so we cannot FFT the whole thing at once.
  • We cut it into short frames — about 20 to 25 milliseconds — and slide along with roughly 10 milliseconds of overlap so we never lose a sound at a boundary.
  • Each frame gets a window function, like a Hamming window, that tapers its edges to zero before the FFT — this avoids artefacts. Frame, window, transform, repeat.

Slide 19 — Part 2 — Turning Audio into Features
  • Part 2, about 22 minutes. We convert the spectrogram into features that match how humans hear and that models love.
  • The Mel scale, the Mel spectrogram Whisper consumes, MFCCs and the classic pipeline, then the open-source toolkit.
  • This is the audio version of Day 1 wrangling — making the data model-ready.

Slide 20 — Compact features beat a million samples
  • Why not feed raw audio to the model? Because millions of raw samples are mostly redundant, and models learn far better from a compact, meaningful representation.
  • Feature extraction is the audio version of Day 1 wrangling: we shrink and shape the signal into something informative.
  • The spectrogram was step one; now we squeeze it into features tuned to speech.

Slide 21 — Frequencies the way ears hear them
  • Human hearing is not linear. We are very sensitive to differences at low frequencies and increasingly less so at high ones.
  • The Mel scale warps the frequency axis to match that perception — equal steps in Mel sound equally far apart to a human.
  • So instead of raw hertz, we work in Mel, because it concentrates resolution where speech information and our ears actually live.

Slide 22 — A Mel filterbank, weighted like the ear
  • Combine the two ideas. Take the spectrogram and pass it through a Mel filterbank — a set of overlapping triangular filters, narrow at low frequencies, wide at high.
  • Each filter sums the energy in its band, giving a perceptually-weighted picture. Whisper uses exactly this: an 80-channel log-Mel spectrogram.
  • Watch the triangles overlay the spectrum. This Mel spectrogram is the bridge from signal processing to Whisper.

Slide 23 — 13 numbers that capture the spectral shape
  • Before deep learning, the workhorse feature was the MFCC — Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients.
  • It is a very compact summary of the spectral shape of each frame, typically just 13 numbers.
  • Thirteen coefficients per frame, instead of hundreds of frequency bins — that compactness made classical speech systems possible.

Slide 24 — The canonical recipe, stage by stage
  • Here is the canonical recipe, stage by stage. Pre-emphasis boosts the quiet high frequencies. Frame and window, as we saw. FFT to get the spectrum.
  • Apply the Mel filterbank, take the log to mimic loudness perception, then a DCT to decorrelate and compress.
  • Keep the first thirteen coefficients and you have your MFCCs. This exact pipeline powered speech recognition for decades.

Slide 25 — Capture how the sound changes
  • Static MFCCs describe a single instant but miss motion — and speech is all about motion.
  • So we add deltas: the first derivative captures how each coefficient is changing, and the second derivative, the delta-delta, captures acceleration.
  • Stack the 13 MFCCs with their deltas and delta-deltas and you get a 39-dimensional vector per frame — static shape plus dynamics.

Slide 26 — Hand-designed features vs learned ones
  • When do you reach for which? MFCCs are tiny, fast and interpretable — still excellent for lightweight tasks like keyword spotting and speaker ID on modest hardware.
  • Modern deep models like Whisper and wav2vec2 skip hand-designed features: they learn their own representations directly from the Mel spectrogram.
  • Rule of thumb: small, on-device, or simple task — MFCC. Accuracy and language coverage at scale — a learned model.

Slide 27 — What you'll actually call
  • The open-source toolkit you will actually call. librosa is the go-to for loading audio and computing spectrograms, Mel and MFCCs.
  • torchaudio gives you tensors, transforms and datasets that plug into PyTorch. soundfile and SoX handle file I/O and resampling.
  • And webrtcvad does voice-activity detection — finding where speech actually is. Every one is open-source and self-hostable.

Slide 28 — Part 3 — From Audio to Words — Whisper
  • Part 3, about 20 minutes. We turn the Mel spectrogram into text with Whisper — open-source, MIT-licensed, self-hostable.
  • It is an encoder-decoder transformer, so everything from Day 1 applies. We trace the full pipeline and the honest caveats.
  • End with a live, open-source demo: load, spectrogram, transcribe.

Slide 29 — Audio in, text out
  • The task is Automatic Speech Recognition, ASR: audio in, text out.
  • That transcript is the front door to everything from Day 1 — once a call is text, you can search it, classify intent, score sentiment, and check compliance.
  • ASR is the bridge from the audio world into the text and LLM world we already know.

Slide 30 — Open, private, and multilingual
  • Why Whisper specifically for a bank. The decisive point: it is open-source, MIT-licensed, with open weights — so we self-host it and customer audio never leaves OCBC.
  • It is robust to accents and background noise, and multilingual across about 99 languages, including Bahasa Indonesia and English.
  • And one model handles both transcription and translation. Open, private, multilingual — exactly our constraints.

Slide 31 — A transformer that reads spectrograms
  • What is Whisper, in Day 1 terms? An encoder-decoder transformer — the very same architecture family as the LLMs we studied.
  • It was trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual audio paired with transcripts.
  • So everything you learned about attention, encoders and decoders applies directly here. Whisper is a transformer that happens to read spectrograms instead of text.

Slide 32 — Spectrogram in, text tokens out
  • This pipeline ties the whole day together. Audio is split into 30-second chunks and converted to an 80-channel log-Mel spectrogram — the exact representation we built in Parts 1 and 2.
  • The encoder reads that spectrogram; the decoder generates text tokens one at a time, with special tokens for language ID, timestamps, and transcribe-versus-translate.
  • The key payoff: the input to Whisper is the Mel spectrogram you now know how to make.

Slide 33 — Pick by accuracy vs latency vs hardware
  • Whisper comes in a ladder of sizes, from tiny at 39 million parameters up to large at about 1.5 billion.
  • Bigger is more accurate but slower and heavier. You pick based on accuracy versus latency versus the hardware you can spare.
  • Note the large model is multilingual-only. For many call-centre tasks, base or small is a great balance.

Slide 34 — A strong baseline, not magic
  • Honest framing — Whisper is a strong baseline, not magic. It struggles with heavy overlap and multiple speakers talking at once.
  • It does not tell you who spoke when; that is diarization, added separately. Real-time streaming needs extra engineering, and self-hosting at scale costs GPU.
  • The open ecosystem helps: whisper.cpp runs on-device, faster-whisper speeds inference, and you can fine-tune for product names and local accents.

Slide 35 — Load → spectrogram → Whisper → text
  • The demo, fully open-source and local. Press R or Replay. Cell 1 loads the call and resamples to 16 kHz with librosa — one line.
  • Cell 2 computes the 80-band Mel spectrogram, the exact input Whisper wants. Cell 3 loads the base model and transcribes, returning text with timestamps.
  • The whole path — load, spectrogram, Whisper, text — runs on our own hardware. Nothing leaves the bank.
