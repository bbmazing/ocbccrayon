12
The Nyquist theorem

Sample at least twice the frequency

  • To capture a frequency, sample at ≥ 2× it.
  • 16 kHz captures up to 8 kHz — enough for clear speech.
  • Phones at 8 kHz capture only 4 kHz → voices sound thin.

Bit depth & quantization

Each sample is rounded to a digital level

  • Sampling sets timing; bit depth sets precision.
  • Each sample is rounded to a level — quantisation.
  • 16-bit standard — 65,536 levels, low noise.

Framing & windowing

Chop into short, overlapping frames

  • Speech is stable only in short slices → ~20–25 ms frames.
  • Slide with ~10 ms overlap — don't lose boundary sounds.
  • Apply a window (Hamming) to taper edges before FFT.

The Mel scale

Frequencies the way ears hear them

  • We hear pitch non-linearly — keen low, blunt high.
  • The Mel scale warps frequency to match perception.
  • More resolution where speech lives — not raw Hz.
Mel (y) vs frequency in Hz (x) — compressed up high
OCBC CRAYON · Audio Data Analytics
21
Mel spectrogram

A Mel filterbank, weighted like the ear

  • Overlapping triangular filters — narrow low, wide high.
  • Each sums energy in its band → ~80 Mel bands.
  • Exactly what Whisper consumes: 80-channel log-Mel.
Triangular Mel filters over the spectrum

24
Deltas (Δ, ΔΔ)

Capture how the sound changes

  • Static MFCCs miss motion — but speech is motion.
  • Δ = first derivative (rate of change).
  • ΔΔ = second derivative (acceleration).
  • 13 + Δ + ΔΔ = a 39-dim vector per frame.
MFCC track (red) and its derivative (blue)
