The agent loop

Think → Act → Observe — like cooking and tasting

Act

  • Think — decide the next small step.
  • Act — actually do it.
  • Observe — look at what happened.

07
When is it an agent job?

Three signs a job needs an agent

It has several steps

Not one fact, but a little errand: do this, then that, then check.

It needs real actions

Something changes in the world — a booking, a payment, a message sent.

The result is worth checking

Did it work? The agent looks, and continues only if it really did.

One fact? A chatbot is enough. A small errand with steps? That's the job for an agent.

several stepsit must choosean action to check

An agent is a heavy tool — don't reach for it when a simple answer will do.

When a step goes wrong

It notices a failure — and tries a fix

Act
pay_bill("electricity", SGD 86.40)
Observe
Declined — not enough in the main account.
Think
Don't give up. There's a backup account — try paying from that instead.
Act
pay_bill("electricity", SGD 86.40, from="savings")
Observe
Paid this time. Reply: “Done — paid from your savings account.”
Because it sees the result, it can recover — instead of failing silently.

14
Start simple

Keep it simple — add helpers only when you must

Most of the time: one helper

A single assistant, a few tools, and a human for the big calls. This handles nearly every useful job — keep it here.

Only for big jobs: a team

A few helpers share a very big task. Strong, but harder to follow — do not add it before you need it.

Simple is good — the fewer parts, the easier it is to trust and follow.

A quick trust check

Three questions before you trust an agent

Just the right tools?

It should have only what the job needs — no power it doesn't require.

A human on the big moves?

Anything large or hard to undo waits for a person to say yes.

Can you see what it did?

Every step is visible afterwards, so you can check its work.

Three yeses and you have a helper you can rely on — any no, fix it first.

Why a human helps
