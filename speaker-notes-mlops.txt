Slide 1 — Machine Learning 101 & MLOps Basics
  • Welcome back. This is Session 2 — it assumes Topic 1 (Python, wrangling, analytics). We move from describing data to making it predict.
  • 90 minutes, intuition-first: no calculus. Two animated code demos, and a banking flavour throughout — fraud, AML, credit, recommendations.
  • Tell them the goal: by the end they can read an ML project end to end and talk to data scientists in their own language.

Slide 2 — From exploring data to making it predict
  • Anchor the new material in the familiar workflow. They already do Ask, Acquire, Wrangle, Explore from Topic 1.
  • Today is the highlighted node — Model — plus what nobody teaches: keeping it alive in production (that is the MLOps half).
  • One sentence bridge: wrangling made the data trustworthy; now we let it predict.

Slide 3 — Learning patterns, not hand-coding rules
  • Plain definition: ML learns patterns from data to make predictions, instead of a human hand-coding the rules.
  • The contrast is the whole point. Rules-based fraud detection is a maintenance nightmare and always one step behind.
  • A learned model generalises from examples and improves as you feed it more labeled data — the same fraud task, a fundamentally different approach.

Slide 4 — Four anchor examples we return to all session
  • These four flagship OCBC use cases are the running examples for the whole deck. Name each and where it lives in the bank.
  • Recommendation lives in the app investment tab; AML and fraud watch transactions; credit scores loan applications.
  • Flag now: fraud, AML and credit are supervised (we have past labeled outcomes); recommendation leans unsupervised. We will formalise that next.

Slide 5 — The loop the rest of the talk follows
  • This is the ML lifecycle in miniature: Frame, Data, Train, Evaluate, Deploy, Monitor.
  • Section 2 zooms into Evaluate (metrics). Section 3 zooms into Deploy and Monitor (MLOps).
  • Emphasise the arrow back: a deployed model is never done — drift sends you back to data and retraining.

Slide 6 — Part 1 — Supervised vs Unsupervised
  • Part 1, about 25 minutes. The single most useful mental model in ML: the supervised / unsupervised split.
  • Keep it concrete by slotting the four anchor cases onto the split as we go.
  • Mention reinforcement learning exists in one line, then declare it out of scope.

Slide 7 — Do you have labels? That decides everything
  • The one mental model to keep: labeled data goes to supervised; no labels and you want structure goes to unsupervised.
  • Slot the anchors: fraud, AML and credit are supervised — we have past labeled outcomes. Recommendation and segmentation are often unsupervised first.
  • Reinforcement learning gets one sentence and a wave — not today.

Slide 8 — Rows, features (X), and a label (y)
  • Shared vocabulary for everything after. Rows are examples (here, customers). Columns are features, capital X.
  • One special column is the label or target, lower-case y — the thing we predict. Here it is the fraud flag.
  • Supervised learning is just: learn the mapping from X to y. Keep pointing back to this table.

Slide 9 — Supervised learning has two flavors
  • Supervised splits into classification (discrete labels) and regression (continuous numbers).
  • Classification: fraud or not, alert or clear, approve or decline. Regression: a credit limit, a probability of default.
  • Note credit approval uses both — a regression score for risk, then a classification decision to approve or decline.

Slide 10 — Classification draws a boundary
  • Classification, visually: points are transactions; the model finds a line (or curve) that best separates fraud from legit.
  • Do not teach the math. Name the common models in one breath — logistic regression, trees, random forests, gradient boosting.
  • A new transaction is classified by which side of the boundary it falls on.

Slide 11 — Regression fits a line to predict a number
  • Regression fits a continuous relationship. Animate the line settling through the cloud of points.
  • Anchor on credit: predict a number — a credit limit or a probability of default.
  • That number is what feeds the approve/decline gate and risk-based pricing — the link to the loan-approval anchor.

Slide 12 — No labels — find the structure
  • Unsupervised has no labels; the goal is structure. Two workhorses: clustering and dimensionality reduction.
  • Clustering groups similar customers — the engine behind segmentation and the investment-tab recommendations.
  • Anomaly detection falls out of this: a transaction in no normal cluster is suspicious — the AML link.

Slide 13 — Cluster customers, then recommend
  • Animate scattered customers snapping into colour-coded segments — the k-means intuition, no math.
  • The big idea: real recommendation blends families. Segments (unsupervised) plus collaborative filtering plus a supervised propensity model.
  • Same clustering view also gives anomaly detection for AML — anything far from every cluster is worth a look.

Slide 14 — Which family? Two questions decide
  • A simple decision aid: first ask if you have labels; then ask what the question is — a value, a class, or groups.
  • Walk the four anchors as worked answers so the framework sticks.
  • Recommendation is the honest one — real systems blend supervised and unsupervised.

Slide 15 — A model is .fit() then .predict()
  • Demo 1, replayed in-deck — press R or Replay to run it again. The point lands in one line: a model is fit then predict.
  • Cell 1 splits data 80/20 so we can grade honestly later. Cell 2 trains a random forest. Cell 3 predicts on unseen rows.
  • Do not dwell on the algorithm — emphasise the shape of the API. This is identical across almost every sklearn model.

Slide 16 — Part 2 — Model Evaluation Metrics
  • Part 2, about 28 minutes — the most practically useful section. The metric you pick changes the whole story.
  • We climb a ladder: accuracy is a lie, then confusion matrix, precision/recall, F1, ROC-AUC, and finally overfitting.
  • Keep grounding every metric in the fraud anchor — rare events break naive accuracy.

Slide 17 — A useless model can score 98%
  • The hook. A model that predicts never-fraud is about 98 percent accurate because fraud is rare — and it catches nothing.
  • Accuracy is misleading on imbalanced problems, which is most interesting banking problems: fraud, AML, default.
  • This sets up why we need precision and recall — metrics that look at the rare class.

Slide 18 — Never grade a model on data it trained on
  • Golden rule of evaluation: hold out data. A model graded on what it memorised always looks better than it is.
  • Animate the split — 80 percent to train, 20 percent held back to test. The test set is your honest estimate.
  • Mention cross-validation as the more robust version; we use it in Demo 2.

Slide 19 — Three splits: learn, tune, then judge
  • Extend train/test to three sets: train fits the model, validation tunes it, and test is the final honest exam — touched once, at the very end.
  • The cardinal sin is tuning against the test set; that leaks it and inflates the score. Keep it locked until you are completely done.
  • Cross-validation rotates the validation fold so one unlucky split cannot fool you — the robust default whenever you tune.

Slide 20 — The confusion matrix: four outcomes
  • The 2x2 that everything derives from. In banking terms: caught fraud (TP), false alarm (FP), missed fraud (FN), correct clear (TN).
  • Animate the cells filling in. Point out FN — missed fraud — is usually the expensive mistake.
  • Precision, recall, F1, ROC all come from these four numbers. Get comfortable here.

Slide 21 — Precision vs recall — two questions
  • Plain words first. Precision is about the alerts you raised — what fraction were right. Recall is about all the real fraud — what fraction you caught.
  • Show which cells each uses: precision is the predicted-fraud column (TP+FP), recall is the actual-fraud row (TP+FN).
  • Different costs pull them apart, which is the trade-off on the next slide.

Slide 22 — Move the threshold, trade precision for recall
  • This is interactive — drag the slider. As the cutoff rises, precision climbs and recall falls, and vice versa.
  • Tie it to the business: in fraud/AML the cost is false alarms versus missed losses; in lending it is bad approvals versus lost good customers.
  • F1 is the harmonic mean — one balanced number when you cannot decide which way to lean.

Slide 23 — ROC curve and AUC
  • ROC sweeps every threshold and plots true-positive rate against false-positive rate. Animate the curve drawing in.
  • AUC summarises it: 0.5 is a coin flip, 1.0 is perfect. It measures how well the model ranks fraud above legit.
  • Important caveat for the bank: on very rare events the precision-recall curve is more honest than ROC.

Slide 24 — Metrics for predicting a number
  • For regression you measure error size, not right-or-wrong. MAE is the average miss in real units — most intuitive.
  • RMSE penalises large errors more, which matters when a few big misses are expensive. R-squared is variance explained.
  • Tie back to the credit anchor: this is how you grade the probability-of-default model feeding loan approval.

Slide 25 — Overfitting and the bias-variance trade-off
  • The core failure mode. Underfitting is too simple. Overfitting memorises noise — perfect on training, bad on new data.
  • Animate the U: training error keeps falling, but total error on new data turns back up. The minimum is the sweet spot.
  • Cross-validation is the robust way to find that spot without fooling yourself.

Slide 26 — Fewer, stronger features beat more features
  • More columns is not more signal — irrelevant features add noise, cost, overfitting risk, and hurt explainability.
  • Three families: filter (correlation), wrapper (recursive feature elimination), embedded (tree importance, L1/Lasso).
  • Banking reality: start with hundreds of engineered signals, ship the handful that actually move fraud risk.

Slide 27 — Hyperparameters: search for the best settings
  • Parameters are learned during training; hyperparameters — tree depth, number of trees, learning rate, regularisation — are chosen before it.
  • They control over- versus under-fitting; recall the depth-12 run that overfit. Explore the space with grid, random, or Bayesian search.
  • Score every candidate on the validation set or via cross-validation, never the test set — the same leakage rule as the three-way split.

Slide 28 — SHAP: explain a single prediction
  • A strong score is not enough in a regulated bank — you must explain why a given decision came out the way it did.
  • SHapley Additive exPlanations attribute one prediction to its features: which signals pushed this case toward fraud, and which pulled it back.
  • The same mechanism answers "why was this loan declined?" — a black-box score becomes an auditable, customer-ready reason, linking to the Part 3 governance checkpoints.

Slide 29 — Same model, different metric, different story
  • Demo 2 evaluates the Demo-1 model. Same model — watch the story change with the metric.
  • The confusion matrix and classification report expose the truth: high accuracy and AUC, but fraud recall is poor.
  • This is the payoff of the whole section: always ask which metric, and whether it matches the business cost.

Slide 30 — Part 3 — MLOps Fundamentals
  • Part 3, about 20 minutes. The half that beginners skip and production teams obsess over.
  • MLOps is how a model leaves the notebook and stays useful — deployment, monitoring, retraining, governance.
  • Keep it bank-flavoured: regulated, audited, and never set-and-forget.

Slide 31 — The notebook is not the finish line
  • Blunt truth: a notebook model delivers zero value. Value starts when it runs reliably against real transactions.
  • Define MLOps: the discipline that blends ML, software engineering, DevOps and data engineering.
  • Frame the rest of the section as crossing that arrow — and staying on the right side of it.

Slide 32 — Why ML is harder than normal software
  • Traditional software is deterministic — you test it once and it behaves. ML breaks that assumption.
  • An ML system depends on data and a learned model as well as code, and the data keeps changing under you.
  • So MLOps manages three interlocking moving parts: data, model, code. The gears turning together is the mental image.

Slide 33 — The MLOps lifecycle is a loop
  • The canonical loop: Design, Data, Train, Evaluate, Deploy, Monitor — and Monitor feeds back to retraining.
  • Animate it advancing node by node. The key idea is the feedback arrow: deployment is the middle, not the end.
  • This loop is the spine of the whole section; the next slides zoom into pieces of it.

Slide 34 — Track experiments, version everything
  • ML has too many runs to track in your head. Log parameters and metrics for every run automatically.
  • Reproducibility means versioning all three: data, model, and code. Run 43 should be re-creatable months later.
  • Name tools lightly — MLflow or Weights and Biases for tracking and a registry, DVC or Git for versioning.

Slide 35 — How a model reaches users
  • Two main serving modes. Real-time API scores each event — fraud at the moment of payment, recommendations in the app.
  • Batch scores on a schedule — overnight AML screening and credit risk runs. Pick by how fresh the decision must be.
  • Before full launch, de-risk with shadow (run silently and compare) and canary (a small traffic slice first).

Slide 36 — Models decay — monitor and retrain
  • The part beginners miss: models decay. Two kinds of drift — data drift (inputs move) and concept drift (the relationship moves).
  • Performance slides quietly below acceptable. You only catch it if you monitor metrics and data and alert on them.
  • Then you retrain — the bump back up. OCBC examples: spending shifts stale the fraud model; new products dull recommendations.

Slide 37 — In a regulated bank, the loop has checkpoints
  • A bank-relevant note, not a compliance lecture. In a regulated environment the lifecycle carries checkpoints.
  • Explainability is the headline: if you decline a loan, you must be able to say why. Fairness and audit trails matter too.
  • Human-in-the-loop oversight stays on the highest-stakes calls — AML and credit. These set expectations for production ML here.

Slide 38 — Three things to remember
  • Replay the two spines: the workflow Frame-to-Monitor, and the metric ladder from confusion matrix up to ROC-AUC.
  • Land the three takeaways: pick the right metric, beware overfitting, treat the model as a product that needs maintenance.
  • These three are what separates a notebook experiment from a system the bank can trust.

Slide 39 — The takeaway
  • Closing callback to how we framed the session: a model is a simplification, judged by usefulness, not truth.
  • For a bank, a fraud model misses some cases and false-alarms on others — we accept that and optimise for business value, while monitoring it.
  • Leads straight into the recap-free close and Q&A.

  • Close the loop: they can now read an ML project end to end and talk to data scientists in their language.
  • Pointers to go deeper — Andrew Ng for the math foundations, an MLOps hands-on track for the production side.
