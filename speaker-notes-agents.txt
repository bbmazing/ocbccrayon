OCBC CRAYON 2026 — Agentic AI Paradigms
Speaker notes · 35 slides
================================================

Slide 1 — Agentic AI Paradigms
  • So far you have seen AI that talks. A chatbot answers your questions. Today we meet AI that does things. We call it an agent. It takes actions and checks the result.
  • The whole talk uses one simple picture. Do a step. See what happened. Then do the next step. It is like cooking and tasting as you go.
  • By the end you can explain how a chatbot and an agent are different. You will also see why a person still says yes to the big moves.

Slide 2 — The big idea
  • This is the one idea for the whole talk. An agent is an assistant that can take real actions. It does not just write a reply.
  • Hold on to this pair of words: talking and doing. Every slide today looks at it in a new way.
  • Keep it friendly. Think of it as a very clever writer that now has a pair of hands.

Slide 3 — One describes the steps; the other does them
  • Here is a picture of the two. Ask a chatbot how to book a trip to Tokyo. It writes you a neat list of steps, then stops. You still have to do all the clicking.
  • An agent gets the same request and really does it. It looks at flights, picks one, books it, and tells you it is done. It acts. It does not just describe.
  • That jump is the whole topic. We move from telling you the steps to doing the steps for you.

Slide 4 — Part 1 — How an Agent Works
  • Part one is how an agent works inside. It is simpler than it sounds. There is one habit it repeats, and a set of hands.
  • We will use easy pictures the whole way. Cooking and tasting. Doing small errands. Giving an assistant hands.
  • No maths. No hard words. Just the simple shape of how it thinks and acts.

Slide 5 — Think → Act → Observe — like cooking and tasting
  • This is the heart of every agent. Think of cooking. You add a little salt. That is act. You taste it. That is observe. Then you decide the next step. That is think. You go round and round until the food is right.
  • An agent does the same thing. It thinks of the next step. It does one step. It looks at what happened. Then it thinks again. It keeps going until the goal is reached.
  • This loop is the most important idea in the whole topic. Watch it go round.

Slide 6 — Walk one turn of the loop, slowly
  • Let us slow the loop right down and watch one turn. Say the goal is 'pay my water bill'. First the agent thinks of the next small step.
  • Then it acts: it pays the bill. Then it observes: it reads back whether the payment went through. Only after looking does it think again about what to do next.
  • That is one full turn of the loop. Think, act, observe, then think again. The agent just keeps turning this wheel until the goal is reached.

Slide 7 — Every step gives it a reality check
  • Why does this loop work so well? Every step shows the agent what is real. It is like tasting, which tells the cook the truth.
  • The agent does not guess the whole answer at once. It does one step. It sees the real result. If the step went wrong, it fixes it.
  • This is the trick for long jobs with many steps. The real result keeps the agent honest at every step.

Slide 8 — A good agent looks before it leaps
  • Here is a quick way to feel the difference. A good agent does one small step. Then it looks before it moves on. A clumsy one tries to do it all at once, with no looking.
  • The good cook tastes after each bit of salt. The bad cook pours in the whole jar and hopes. It is the same idea here. Check, then go on.
  • So the loop is not just a nice habit. It is what makes an agent you can trust, not one that goes wrong.

Slide 9 — “Book me a trip to Tokyo next month”
  • Now watch the loop on a bigger job. Book me a trip to Tokyo next month. Read it from top to bottom. Think, act, observe, again and again.
  • Notice it does not plan the whole trip at once. It looks for flights. It sees the prices. It picks one. It books it. Then it checks the booking really went through.
  • This is the same simple habit as before. It is just used on a longer, more real job.

Slide 10 — Three signs a job needs an agent
  • When is a job an agent job, and not just a question? Here is a simple test. Does it have many steps, real actions, and a result worth a check?
  • Book a trip. Pay a bill, then check the balance. Get a refund. These all pass the test. But “what is the capital of Japan?” does not. That is just a question.
  • Use this as a quick test. One fact? A chatbot is fine. A small errand with steps? That is the job for an agent.

Slide 11 — Sometimes an agent is the wrong tool
  • Just as important: knowing when not to use an agent. An agent is a heavy tool. For many jobs it is too much, and a plain answer is better and safer.
  • Do not use an agent for a single fact, like 'what time do you open?'. Do not use one when there is only one fixed path, like a set form. And do not use one when a wrong move would be hard to undo and there is no human to check.
  • Reach for an agent only when there are real steps, real choices, and a result worth a look. Otherwise keep it simple.

Slide 12 — Tools let the assistant actually do things
  • So the agent can think. But thinking alone cannot pay a bill or send a message. It needs hands. We call those hands tools.
  • A tool lets the assistant do one real thing. Look something up. Send a message. Make a payment. More tools means it can do more.
  • Two small extras finish the picture. It remembers what was said earlier in the chat. And for big jobs, a few helpers can share the work.

Slide 13 — It remembers, and it can ask for help
  • Two small helpers to know. First is memory. The assistant remembers what was said earlier in the same chat. So you do not have to say it again.
  • Second is teamwork. For a big job, a few helpers can share the work. One finds the facts. One checks them. It is like a small team.
  • Keep it light. Most of the time one assistant is enough. The team idea is only for very big jobs.

Slide 14 — Part 2 — How It Uses a Tool
  • Part two shows how the assistant uses a tool. Then a short demo, so the idea feels real.
  • Here is the calming part. The assistant never takes control. It just asks. Plain software does the action.
  • We keep it simple with one example: check my balance.

Slide 15 — The assistant requests; software does the work
  • This part surprises people. The assistant never does anything by itself. It just says: please run check-balance for account A-1029.
  • Plain software joins the assistant to its tools. The software runs the real action. Then it gives back the answer. The assistant reads it and goes on.
  • This hand-off is why it stays safe. The assistant can only ask. It can never take control.

Slide 16 — Watch one tool, one loop
  • A short demo, played for you. This is the smallest agent you can build. One tool, one loop. It does just what we said.
  • Watch the four steps. We give it one tool: check a balance. It asks to use that tool. The software runs it for real. Then the assistant reads the result and writes the answer.
  • Press R to play it again. This is every agent in small form. Ask, run the tool, read the result, answer.

Slide 17 — “Pay my bill, then tell me my balance”
  • Now see the loop on a real job. Pay my electricity bill, then tell me my new balance. Read it from top to bottom. Think, act, observe, again.
  • Notice the assistant does not guess. It pays. Then it checks the real balance after. So the number it gives you is true.
  • This is the whole topic in one screen. A chatbot could only tell you the steps. The agent really does them.

Slide 18 — For each step, it picks the tool that fits
  • Most real jobs need more than one tool. The assistant picks the right one each time. It is like a worker who picks the right tool from a tool belt.
  • Here it has three tools. Look up a fact. Send a message. Make a payment. For each step, it picks the one that fits. Then it asks the software to run it.
  • The lesson is simple. Give an assistant only the few tools the job needs. Then it picks the right one at the right time.

Slide 19 — Same job, same tools — very different agents
  • Let us make good and bad feel real with one job: 'book me a hotel for next week'. Both agents have the same tools. The difference is how they work.
  • The good agent checks the dates first, sees the room is free, books it, then reads back the confirmation number. It looks before and after each step. The bad agent books the first room it sees, never checks the dates, and never confirms it worked. So nobody knows if the booking is even real.
  • Same tools, very different agents. The good one is careful and checks; the bad one rushes and hopes. The loop is what makes the difference.

Slide 20 — It notices a failure — and tries a fix
  • What happens when a step goes wrong? This is where the loop really helps. The assistant tries something. It sees it failed. Then it tries a smart fix. It does not just give up.
  • Here a payment is turned down. The assistant does not stop. It notices, moves to the backup account, and the next try works.
  • It is like a cook who tastes the soup and finds it too salty. So the cook adds water, not serves it as is. Seeing the result is what makes the fix possible.

Slide 21 — It remembers, so you don't repeat yourself
  • Memory makes the assistant feel more human. It remembers what was said earlier in the same chat. So you can talk in a normal way and not repeat things.
  • Watch this. You name your account once. Later you just say “pay it from there.” And it knows which account “there” means.
  • That is all memory means here. It holds the thread of the chat, the way a good helper does.

Slide 22 — “Did my pay come in? If so, move $200 to savings”
  • Here is one more everyday story, end to end. The request is: 'Did my pay come in yet? If it did, move two hundred to savings.' This has a check, a choice, and an action.
  • The agent reads the recent money in. It sees the salary did arrive. Because it did, it moves the two hundred across. Then it reads the savings balance back to be sure it landed.
  • Notice the 'if'. The agent only moves the money because the pay really came in. It checked first. That is the loop helping it do the right thing.

Slide 23 — Same hand-off, even when it changes things
  • One more look at the hand-off. This time the tool changes something. The assistant asks: please send the email. It does not open an inbox itself.
  • Plain software sends the email. Then it reports back: sent. The assistant reads that and tells you it is done.
  • It is the same shape as a look-up. Ask, the tool runs for real, the answer comes back. Whether it reads or does, the assistant only asks.

Slide 24 — Part 3 — Staying in Control
  • Part three is the most important. It is how we keep all of this safe. The power to take actions is also the power to make a costly mistake.
  • Here is the simple rule. A person says yes to anything big or risky before it happens.
  • Short and useful. Just the key points for staying in control.

Slide 25 — A human approves the important moves
  • This is the one safety rule that matters. The assistant can get things ready and suggest a step. But a person says yes before anything big or risky happens.
  • Pay a small bill you set up? Fine on its own. Move a large sum, or do anything you cannot undo? A person says yes first.
  • Think of the assistant as a smart new helper who prepares the work. A person signs off on the big choices. That keeps you in control.

Slide 26 — “Chase a refund for my faulty order”
  • Here is the safety rule at work. The customer asks the assistant to get a refund for a broken order. This job has many steps and a real result.
  • The assistant does the hard part. It finds the order. It writes the refund request. It gets it all ready. Then it stops at the step that matters.
  • It does not send the request on its own. It shows the customer the draft and waits for a yes. The assistant prepares. The person says yes.

Slide 27 — Keep it simple — add helpers only when you must
  • Here is a useful design tip. Start simple. Most jobs need just one assistant with a few tools. Add more only when the job really needs it.
  • On the left is the normal case. One helper, a few tools, a person to say yes to the big moves. This covers nearly all useful agents.
  • On the right is the rare case. A very big job where a few helpers share the work. Adding all that too soon just makes things hard to trust and follow.

Slide 28 — Three questions before you trust an agent
  • Before you trust an agent with a real job, ask a few questions. These three catch most of the trouble.
  • Does it have only the tools it needs? Does a person say yes to the risky moves? And can you see what it did, step by step, after?
  • If all three are yes, you have a helper you can trust. If any one is no, fix it first.

Slide 29 — An OCBC assistant prepares; a human decides
  • Now a quick bank example. An OCBC assistant could look into a strange charge. It pulls the recent payments, spots an odd payment from abroad, and gets a case ready.
  • But it does not block your card by itself. It gives the choice to a person, just as our safety rule says.
  • This is only an example. But the shape is right. The assistant does the hard work. A person makes the call that matters.

Slide 30 — “Could I easily undo this?”
  • Here is an easy way to decide what an agent may do alone. Ask: could I easily undo this? If yes, green light. If no, it waits for a person.
  • Green-light actions are small and easy to undo. Read a balance. Write a note. Pay a tiny set-up bill. The assistant just does them.
  • Red-light actions are big or final. A large transfer. Close an account. Block a card. These always wait for a person to say yes.

Slide 31 — Three reasons to keep a person in the loop
  • Why keep a person in the loop at all? Three plain reasons, easy to remember.
  • It catches honest mistakes before they cost money. It keeps a person in charge of the big calls. And it builds trust, because people can see a person is watching.
  • None of this slows down the everyday stuff. The small, easy-to-undo actions still go fast. The person only steps in where it counts.

Slide 32 — The same loop, three everyday errands
  • Before we wrap up, here are three small stories together, the kind of thing an agent does well. Each one has steps, an action, and a check.
  • Pay a bill and confirm the new balance. Chase a refund for a broken order. Book a trip with a flight and a hotel. Same simple loop behind all three: think, act, observe, repeat.
  • Notice the pattern. Every one of these is a little errand, not just a question. That is exactly the kind of job an agent is for.

Slide 33 — Chatbot vs agent, at a glance
  • Before the recap, one clear side-by-side. Same request, two very different things happen. This table is the whole topic on one screen.
  • A chatbot writes a reply and stops. An agent takes actions, checks each result, and keeps going until the job is done. And a person says yes to the big moves.
  • If you remember just one slide, make it this one. Talking and doing, line by line.

Slide 34 — Four things to take away
  • Let us pull it together. An agent is an assistant that does things, not just talks. It takes actions and checks the result.
  • It runs one simple habit, think-act-observe, again and again. Tools are its hands. It remembers the chat. And for big jobs, a few helpers can share the work.
  • And most of all: a person says yes to anything big or risky. That is the whole story.

Slide 35 — From talking to doing
  • Let us close. We went from AI that talks to AI that does things, an agent. We saw the simple loop and the safety rule behind it.
  • Think back to the start. A chatbot talks. An agent acts and checks what happened. You can now explain that difference to anyone.
  • Thank everyone and take their questions.
