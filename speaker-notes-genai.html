Slide 1

Generative AI & LLMs · Theory

  • Welcome to Session 3. This assumes Topics 1 and 2 — you can wrangle data and make it predict. Today: making machines generate language.
  • 90 minutes, intuition-first: no matrix math. One animated demo, banking flavour, and we close by spotting today's theory inside OCBC's own AI products.
  • Promise: by the end you can read a GenAI system end to end and talk to the people building them.
Slide 2

The idea that started it

  • Open on the single most important idea in modern AI — the 2017 paper title. It doubles as a teaser for Part 2.
  • Everything today — GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama — descends from this one architectural idea: attention.
  • We will unpack exactly what attention means, with pictures, in the transformer section.
Slide 3

From predicting data to generating language

  • Bridge from Topic 2. There we made data predict a label or a number. GenAI goes one step further: it generates new content.
  • Predictive ML answers is this fraud? Generative AI writes the suspicious-activity summary. Same data foundation, a new capability.
  • Frame today as adding generation on top of the prediction skills they already have.
Slide 4

Discriminative labels · generative produces

  • The core distinction. Discriminative models draw boundaries and label — that was Topic 2.
  • Generative models produce new content that resembles their training data — text, here.
  • Banking pair: a discriminative model flags a suspicious transaction; a generative model drafts the SAR narrative for the analyst to review.
Slide 5

LLMs are the language engine of GenAI

  • Place LLMs in the landscape. Generative AI is the umbrella — it spans text, images, audio and code.
  • LLMs are the text/language engine of that umbrella. Today is entirely about the text branch.
  • Many bank use cases are multimodal at the edges, but the language model is the core we are studying.
Slide 6

The theory you will see in our own tools

  • One grounding slide before the theory. LLMs are already inside the bank — these three are the running showcase.
  • Document extraction reads KYC forms; an internal assistant helps relationship managers; a customer assistant answers in-app.
  • Promise: every concept today shows up in one of these. We revisit them at the end.
Slide 7

Part 1 — Large Language Models

  • Part 1, about 25 minutes. We define a language model, show next-token prediction, and walk the history.
  • Keep it concrete: tokens, embeddings, scale, the three-stage training recipe, and where LLMs fail.
  • Ends with a live demo of a single API call, then grounding with RAG.
Slide 8

Autocomplete on steroids

  • Plainest possible definition: a model that assigns probabilities to sequences and predicts the next token.
  • The one-liner that sticks: autocomplete on steroids. Your phone keyboard does a tiny version of this.
  • All the apparent intelligence is doing this one thing extremely well, over and over.
Slide 9

It predicts a distribution, then picks

  • Make it concrete. Given the prompt, the model produces a probability for every possible next token.
  • Here the top candidate is balance, then frozen, overdrawn. It samples from this distribution to pick a word.
  • Repeat token by token and you get fluent text. The whole magic is doing this distribution well.
Slide 10

Words become numbers the model can do math on

  • Two ideas. First, text is broken into tokens — roughly word-pieces — because models work in fixed units.
  • Second, each token maps to a vector, an embedding, where similar meanings sit near each other.
  • Why numbers: models do arithmetic, not words. debit and credit land close; king and queen close — geometry captures meaning.
Slide 11

Scale unlocked learning from the prompt

  • Large is three things multiplied: parameters, training data, and compute. GPT-3 was 175 billion parameters.
  • Scale did something surprising — it unlocked in-context learning: the model learns from examples in the prompt, with no retraining.
  • A few abilities appear only past a size threshold — emergent abilities. We exploit in-context learning in the prompting section.
Slide 12

Sixty years to an overnight success

  • Sixty years in one minute. ELIZA faked conversation with rules; statistical NLP counted word frequencies.
  • word2vec (2013) gave words vectors; the Transformer (2017) changed everything; GPT and BERT (2018) started the pretraining era.
  • GPT-3 (2020) showed in-context learning at 175B; ChatGPT (2022) brought it to everyone. Today: GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama — multimodal and reasoning.
Slide 13

Three stages: pretrain, fine-tune, align

  • How a modern LLM is actually made — three stages. Pretraining: predict the next token on massive text. It learns language and world knowledge, but not how to behave.
  • Fine-tuning / instruction-tuning: train on examples of following instructions, so it answers rather than just continues text.
  • RLHF: align to human preferences using feedback — helpful, honest, harmless. This is what makes a raw model into an assistant.
Slide 14

A brilliant drafter, an unreliable oracle

  • Honest framing for a risk-aware audience. Strengths: drafting, summarising, extraction, translation, code.
  • Limits: hallucination — confidently wrong; no live knowledge without retrieval; weak at exact math; can inherit bias.
  • The point: treat output as a capable draft to verify, never as ground truth. This sets up RAG and guardrails.
Slide 15

RAG: retrieve, augment, generate

  • The fix for hallucination and stale knowledge: retrieval-augmented generation. Do not rely on the model's memory.
  • Retrieve relevant passages from a trusted knowledge base, augment the prompt with them, then generate the answer from that context.
  • Banking framing: answer from our policy documents, with citations — not from whatever the model happened to memorise.
Slide 16

The model is one API call

  • Demo 1, replayed in-deck — press R or Replay. The model itself is a single API call.
  • Cell 2 is a bare call: prompt in, completion out. Cell 3 prepends a retrieved policy snippet — the RAG pattern — and the answer becomes grounded and specific.
  • The lesson: the model is one API call; the engineering is the context and prompt you wrap around it.
Slide 17

Part 2 — The Transformer

  • Part 2, about 25 minutes. Pure intuition — attention links, parallel heads, stacked blocks. No matrix math.
  • Start with why older models hit a wall, then the 2017 breakthrough, then self-attention shown visually.
  • End by connecting the architecture back to reading and drafting bank text.
Slide 18

Reading word-by-word hits a wall

  • Set up the problem the transformer solved. Older models — RNNs — read one word at a time, in order.
  • That is slow, and they forget long-range context: by the end of a long paragraph the start has faded.
  • The transformer's move: read the whole sequence at once, in parallel, and let every word see every other.
Slide 19

Attention Is All You Need

  • The landmark. Vaswani and colleagues at Google, 2017. The radical claim in the title: you can drop recurrence entirely.
  • Replace it with attention, and process the sequence in parallel. Simpler, faster, and it scaled far better.
  • This single architecture underlies GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini and Llama. The rest of Part 2 is what is inside it.
Slide 20

Every word weighs every other word

  • The heart of the transformer, shown. Every word looks at every other word and weighs how relevant each is.
  • Here the focus word is charge. Attention links it most strongly to disputed, also to customer and card — it builds context.
  • No matrix math today — just the picture: thicker link means more attention. This is how the model resolves what a word means in context.
Slide 21

Many readers, each tracking one thing

  • One attention pass catches one kind of relationship. Real transformers run many in parallel — heads.
  • Each head learns to track something different: grammar, meaning, or reference. Then their reads are combined.
  • Analogy: a panel of readers, each following one thread, pooling notes for a richer understanding.
Slide 22

What else is in a transformer block

  • Round out the block in plain terms. Positional encoding tags each token with its order, since attention alone is order-blind.
  • Feed-forward layers process each token a bit more on their own. Residual connections and layer norm keep training stable as you stack.
  • Attention plus these pieces is the transformer block — the unit you repeat.
Slide 23

Stack the block, deepen the model

  • Now repeat. Stack many identical blocks and you get a deep network — each layer refines the representation.
  • More layers, more parameters, more data — more capability. GPT-3 stacked 96 of these blocks.
  • This is the concrete meaning of large from Part 1 — depth times width times data.
Slide 24

Encoder understands · decoder generates

  • One clean contrast. Encoder models like BERT read the whole text to understand it — great for classification and search.
  • Decoder models like GPT, Claude and Gemini generate text left to right — great for writing and chat.
  • Most chat LLMs today are decoder-only. That is the family behind the customer assistant.
Slide 25

The same machinery reads our bank text

  • Zoom back out before prompting. The very same attention machinery is what reads bank text and drafts summaries.
  • Read the note, attend to what matters, generate the summary token by token — that is the whole pipeline.
  • This is the bridge to Part 3: now that we have a capable model, how do we steer it?
Slide 26

Part 3 — Prompt Engineering

  • Part 3, about 23 minutes — the most immediately useful section for everyone in the room.
  • We climb the lab-recommended ladder of techniques, with a banking example for each.
  • End on responsible use in a bank: hallucination, injection, privacy, human-in-the-loop.
Slide 27

Steer the model with words, not retraining

  • Define it. Prompt engineering is steering a fixed model with the words you give it — no weight changes.
  • It is far faster and cheaper than fine-tuning, preserves the model's general knowledge, and keeps working across model updates.
  • Reach for prompting first; fine-tuning is a later, heavier tool.
Slide 28

Climb only as high as you need

  • The spine of the section — the lab-recommended ladder, in impact order. Start simple, add only what you need.
  • Be clear and direct, then give examples, then ask for step-by-step reasoning, then add structure, a role, and finally chain prompts.
  • Most tasks are solved by the first two or three rungs. Climb only as far as the problem demands.
Slide 29

Specific beats clever

  • Rung 1, the highest-leverage. Vague prompts get vague answers. Say exactly what you want, with context and motivation.
  • The colleague test: if a new colleague would be confused by your instruction, so is the model.
  • Here, the specific version names the role, the format, and the goal — and gets a usable answer first time.
Slide 30

Show, don't just tell

  • Rung 2. Do not just tell — show. Three to five diverse, canonical examples teach the format and tone better than any description.
  • Banking example: a couple of labelled transaction-to-category pairs, then the new one to classify.
  • The model copies the pattern. Keep examples varied so it generalises rather than memorising.
Slide 31

Ask it to think step by step

  • Rung 3. For multi-step problems, ask the model to reason step by step before answering — accuracy jumps.
  • Also give it permission to say I don't know, which reduces confident guessing.
  • Trade-off: it is slower and uses more tokens, so reserve it for genuinely complex tasks.
Slide 32

Separate the parts, set the persona

  • Rungs 4 and 5 together. Structure your prompt with clear sections or tags so the model never mixes instructions with data.
  • Assign a role — you are a compliance analyst — to set tone, vocabulary and expertise.
  • Tags like context, instructions and data are especially useful when you paste in documents.
Slide 33

Break big tasks into a chain

  • Rung 6. For big tasks, do not ask for everything at once. Break it into a chain of focused steps.
  • Draft, then review, then improve — each step gets the model's full attention, and the self-review catches errors.
  • Chaining trades a little latency for markedly higher quality on complex work.
Slide 34

Use it responsibly in a bank

  • Close Part 3 with responsible use — guardrails, not a compliance lecture. Hallucination: verify facts before acting.
  • Prompt injection: untrusted text can carry hidden instructions, so never trust pasted content blindly.
  • Privacy: no sensitive customer data into unapproved tools. And keep a human in the loop for any real decision.
Slide 35

Showcase — GenAI at OCBC

  • Bring it home. Everything today shows up in OCBC's own AI products.
  • Three illustrative examples, mapped to the exact concepts we covered.
  • Where internal details are unknown, we describe generically and label as illustrative.
Slide 36

Spot today's concepts at OCBC

  • Map each product back to a concept. Document extraction is tokens and embeddings turning forms into structured fields.
  • The RM-support chatbot is RAG plus prompting — grounded answers from policy and product knowledge.
  • The customer assistant is a decoder LLM with grounding and guardrails. Illustrative — real products, simplified.
Slide 37

OCBC runs its own open model, in-house

  • OCBC now runs its own open-weight model in-house: Qwen3.5-35B-A3B, served on NVIDIA H200 GPUs. Open-weights means the parameters are published, so we can download and host the model ourselves instead of calling an external API.
  • The A3B in the name means mixture-of-experts: of about 35 billion parameters, only around 3 billion are active for any given token. You get large-model quality at small-model inference cost and speed, which is why it serves efficiently on H200s, each carrying 141 GB of memory.
  • Why on-premise: customer and bank data never leaves our environment, which is essential for privacy, data residency and regulation. We also gain full control to fine-tune and guardrail, predictable cost at scale, and independence from any single provider.
  • Tie back to the theory: this is still a decoder-only transformer doing next-token prediction. The difference is open weights running on our own hardware.
Slide 38

Three things to remember

  • Replay the three spines. What an LLM is: next-token prediction, trained in three stages.
  • The transformer: attention, processed in parallel, stacked deep. Prompting: the ladder from clear-and-direct up to chaining.
  • Three takeaways: it is prediction not magic; steer with prompts before fine-tuning; verify and use responsibly.
