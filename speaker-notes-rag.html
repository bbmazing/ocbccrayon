Slide 1

Knowledge Retrieval Grounding AI in Your Data

  • Welcome to Session 4, the final core topic. This builds directly on Topic 3 — you know what an LLM is, and that it hallucinates and has no live knowledge.
  • Today we fix that: retrieval. Give the model the right context, from our own documents, at the right moment. 90 minutes, one demo, all banking examples.
  • Promise: by the end you can reason about a RAG system end to end and explain why our chatbots can cite a real policy.
Slide 2

Why retrieval matters

  • The framing principle for the whole session. Capability is not the bottleneck — access to the right information is.
  • A frontier model that cannot see OCBC's product sheets will confidently invent the early-withdrawal penalty. Retrieval is what closes that gap.
  • We will call this back at the close.
Slide 3

From a confident guesser to a grounded one

  • Bridge from Topic 3. We established two hard limits: LLMs hallucinate, and they have no live or internal knowledge — they only know their training data.
  • RAG is the fix. Instead of trusting the model's memory, we hand it the relevant facts at question time.
  • Frame the session as turning a confident guesser into a grounded, citable assistant.
Slide 4

Confident, fluent — and wrong

  • Make the failure visceral. Ask a specific, real OCBC question — the early-withdrawal penalty on a 6-month time deposit.
  • The model never read our product sheet, so it produces a fluent, plausible, and wrong number. That is the danger: it looks authoritative.
  • This is exactly what retrieval prevents — and why a bank cannot ship an ungrounded assistant.
Slide 5

Why retrieval wins for bank knowledge

  • Three ways to give a model knowledge. Fine-tuning retrains the weights — it is great for teaching behaviour, tone or format, but a poor way to add facts: it is expensive, it cannot cite a source, and it is stale the moment a policy changes.
  • Stuffing everything into the prompt hits context limits and gets costly fast — you cannot paste the whole policy library on every question.
  • Retrieval fetches just the relevant passages on demand. For knowledge that is large, changing, and sensitive, RAG wins.
Slide 6

An open-book exam for the model

  • Define it. RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation: find relevant text from a trusted source, add it to the prompt, then generate.
  • The analogy that sticks: an open-book exam instead of a closed-book one. The model does not have to memorise; it looks things up.
  • Three moves — retrieve, augment, generate. The rest of the deck is how each is done well.
Slide 7

Index once, retrieve every time

  • This diagram is the spine of the entire deck. RAG has two phases. Indexing happens offline, once: documents are chunked, embedded, and stored in a vector database.
  • Retrieval happens online, per question: embed the query, search the index, optionally rerank, stuff the top chunks into the prompt, and answer.
  • Every later section zooms into one box: embeddings (Part 1), chunking (Part 2), and the search and rerank mechanics (Part 3) — so if “rerank” looks unfamiliar here, that is fine, it is covered there. Keep returning to this map.
Slide 8

Grounded, private, and auditable

  • Make the bank case explicit. Answers are grounded in our own policies — current and citable, not the model's vague memory.
  • Sensitive documents stay inside our infrastructure; nothing is shipped to a third party. That is decisive in a regulated environment.
  • And every answer can carry a source — less hallucination and real auditability, because you can show where it came from. Be precise though: retrieval reduces hallucination, it does not eliminate it. The model can still misread or ignore the text it is handed, which is exactly why we measure faithfulness later.
Slide 9

Part 1 — Vector Embeddings

  • Part 1, about 22 minutes. Embeddings are the load-bearing idea of retrieval — meaning as coordinates.
  • We anchor on OCBC's actual embedding model, Qwen3-Embedding-8B, and the real tuning levers around it.
  • End on the golden rule: one model, one space, for queries and documents alike.
Slide 10

Text becomes a vector of meaning

  • Recap from Topic 3, now load-bearing — but at a bigger grain. There, each token had its own vector; here a whole chunk of text collapses to a single vector (a “passage embedding”) that captures its overall meaning.
  • The key property: texts with similar meaning get similar vectors, so they sit close together in space.
  • Everything in retrieval reduces to comparing these vectors. Numbers, because computers compare numbers, not words.
Slide 11

Meaning becomes geometry

  • Make it visual. Picture every text as a point in space. Texts about the same thing cluster together.
  • Time deposit and fixed deposit land right next to each other — same concept, different words. Credit card sits far away.
  • Meaning becomes geometry. This is the whole reason retrieval can match on sense rather than exact words.
Slide 12

Match meaning, not exact words

  • Why bother with embeddings over plain keyword search? Keyword search matches exact words, so it misses synonyms and paraphrases.
  • Early withdrawal and breaking your deposit early mean the same thing but share no keywords — keyword search whiffs; embeddings match on meaning.
  • Foreshadow the catch: embeddings can miss exact identifiers like a product code. We fix that later with hybrid search.
Slide 13

Qwen3-Embedding-8B, self-hosted

  • The concrete tool OCBC uses for embeddings: Qwen3-Embedding-8B. Walk the specs that actually matter in practice.
  • 8B parameters means strong semantic quality. The 32K-token context is the most it can embed at once — but for retrieval we deliberately feed it much smaller chunks, because a focused passage matches far more precisely than a whole document (see Chunking, Part 2). The output size is also flexible — from 32 up to 4096 numbers per vector — thanks to Matryoshka representation learning, which we unpack a couple of slides from now.
  • It covers 100+ languages (including Bahasa Indonesia) and code; it is instruction-aware, meaning you can tell it what the text is for; and we self-host it, so sensitive documents never leave our infrastructure.
Slide 14

Top of the multilingual leaderboard

  • Why this model. MTEB — the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark — is the standard scoreboard for embedding models. As of June 2025, Qwen3-Embedding-8B ranked number one on its multilingual leaderboard with a score of 70.58. Present that as a 2025 snapshot and verify the current standing before quoting.
  • Multilingual strength matters for us — Indonesian and English banking documents living in one consistent space.
  • And self-hosting keeps the data inside OCBC. Strong, multilingual, and ours.
Slide 15

Tell the embedder what the query is for

  • A cheap, practical lever. Qwen3 lets you prepend a short task instruction to the query before embedding.
  • For example: Represent this banking-policy question for retrieval. This nudges the vector toward the retrieval task.
  • Typical gain is one to five percent in retrieval quality — small effort, free improvement. Instruct queries; documents usually stay plain.
Slide 16

Trade a little accuracy for big savings

  • Dimension choice is a real tuning decision. Bigger vectors (more numbers each) capture more nuance but cost more storage and slow the search down.
  • Matryoshka representation learning — named after Russian nesting dolls — packs the most important information into the first dimensions. So you can truncate the same vector to fewer dimensions, say 1024, and keep most of the accuracy for a fraction of the cost.
  • Drag the slider: near the top, cutting dimensions saves storage and latency while accuracy barely moves — that is the MRL win. Push it too far down (say 64) and accuracy drops noticeably, so there is no single right value: tune it for your data and budget, and measure.
Slide 17

One model, one space

  • The one rule people break. Queries and documents must be embedded with the same model, so they live in the same geometric space.
  • Mix two models and the coordinates are not comparable — distances become meaningless and retrieval silently degrades.
  • Corollary: if you ever switch embedding models, you must re-embed everything. One model, one space.
Slide 18

Part 2 — Chunking

  • Part 2, about 20 minutes. Chunking is the least glamorous and often highest-leverage knob in RAG.
  • We compare fixed, recursive and semantic splitting, then overlap, metadata, and contextual retrieval.
  • Defaults to remember: recursive, roughly 256 to 512 tokens, 10 to 20 percent overlap.
Slide 19

Split the document into passages

  • Why chunk at all? You cannot embed a 50-page policy as a single vector — the meaning gets averaged into mush and it will not fit retrieval context.
  • So you split the document into passages and embed each one. Now a query can match the specific paragraph that answers it.
  • Chunking is frequently the highest-leverage decision in the whole pipeline — get it wrong and nothing downstream can recover.
Slide 20

Too big, too small, or just right

  • The Goldilocks problem. Chunk too big and the embedding is diluted with off-topic text, so match quality drops.
  • Chunk too small and the fragment loses the context needed to actually answer the question.
  • You want it just right: one coherent idea per chunk. Same passage, three ways — the middle one retrieves best.
Slide 21

Cut every N tokens — the blunt baseline

  • Strategy one: fixed-size. Cut every N tokens — say 256 to 512 — regardless of meaning. Simple and fast.
  • The downside is obvious: it happily slices through the middle of a sentence or a table, ignoring natural boundaries.
  • It is the blunt baseline. Fine to start, but you can almost always do better.
Slide 22

Split along natural boundaries

  • Strategy two: recursive — the pragmatic default. Split along natural boundaries: sections, then paragraphs, then sentences, until chunks fit.
  • It respects the document's structure, so chunks stay coherent. This is the recommended starting point.
  • Defaults to remember: roughly 256 to 512 tokens with 10 to 20 percent overlap.
Slide 23

Cut where the meaning shifts

  • Strategy three: semantic. Embed sentences, then place a boundary wherever the meaning shifts — where cosine distance between neighbours spikes.
  • It produces the most coherent chunks, but it costs an extra embedding pass and yields variable sizes.
  • Higher accuracy in principle — but benchmark it on your own docs before adopting; it is not always worth the cost.
Slide 24

Overlap stops orphans; metadata enables citations

  • Two practical add-ons. Overlap: let adjacent chunks share 10 to 20 percent of text, so a key sentence at a boundary is not orphaned.
  • Metadata: store source doc, section, page and effective date with every chunk.
  • In a bank that metadata is not optional — it is what lets every answer cite a real, dated source for audit.
Slide 25

Give each chunk its context before embedding

  • The best-practice upgrade, from Anthropic. A bare chunk that says the penalty is 1% is context-less — the index does not know which product or year.
  • Contextual retrieval prepends a short context blurb to each chunk before embedding — e.g. “from OCBC's 2025 Time Deposit T&Cs, on early withdrawal”. The blurb is short and on-topic, so it sharpens the match rather than re-diluting the chunk.
  • Anthropic reported around 35 percent fewer retrieval failures from contextual embeddings alone — rising to roughly 49 percent when combined with keyword (BM25) search, and further still once reranking is added. Cheap to add, big win.
Slide 26

Part 3 — Retrieval Mechanics

  • Part 3, about 24 minutes — how retrieval actually works under the hood, intuition only.
  • Cosine similarity, nearest neighbours, approximate search, then the two upgrades that matter: hybrid and reranking.
  • End with the full pipeline and how you measure that it works.
Slide 27

Closeness is the angle between arrows

  • How do we measure closeness? Cosine similarity looks at the angle between two vectors, not their length — a short arrow and a long arrow pointing the same way still count as the same meaning.
  • Score near 1 = almost the same direction = near-identical meaning; near 0 = unrelated. That is all “cosine similarity” means.
  • We compare direction, not length, so a one-line query and a long passage can still score as a strong match. (Length can be skewed by things like how long or repetitive a text is — which we do not want driving the match.) Watch the query vector swing toward the closest chunk; only the direction counts. The 0.97 here is illustrative of a strong but not identical match.
Slide 28

Grab the k closest chunks to the query

  • Retrieval in one line: embed the query, drop it into the same vector space, and grab the k closest chunks — say the top 5.
  • Watch the query land in the cloud; its nearest neighbours light up. Those are the passages we hand to the model.
  • Top-k is a knob: too few and you miss the answer; too many and you add noise and cost.
Slide 29

A smart shortcut through millions of vectors

  • Scale problem: comparing the query against millions of vectors one by one is too slow for a live chatbot.
  • Vector databases use Approximate Nearest Neighbour (ANN) indexes — like HNSW, which links the vectors into a graph you hop across to jump straight to the right neighbourhood — giving near-instant results for a tiny, usually invisible accuracy trade-off.
  • Name a few lightly: pgvector, FAISS, Milvus. The idea to keep: ANN is a smart shortcut, not an exhaustive search.
Slide 30

Semantic search blurs exact tokens

  • Pure semantic search has a real blind spot: exact matches. Embeddings blur precise tokens.
  • Ask for product code TD-6M or a specific clause number, and semantic search may return something that means roughly the same but is the wrong exact item.
  • Error codes, account types, product codes, clause numbers — these need exact matching. That is the motivation for hybrid.
Slide 31

Meaning and exact strings, together

  • The fix is to use both. Hybrid search runs semantic (vector) search and classic keyword search in parallel, then merges the two rankings. BM25 is just the standard keyword-scoring method — it rewards rare, exact words like a product code.
  • A common way to merge them is Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF): it blends the two ranked lists by how high each result sits in each. Semantic catches meaning and paraphrase; keyword nails the exact code or clause number.
  • Hybrid is the current best-practice default — it catches both the sense and the exact string.
Slide 32

Re-read the shortlist for precision

  • Reranking is the precision step. First retrieve a broad shortlist cheaply — say the top 20 candidates.
  • Then a reranker — a model called a cross-encoder — reads each candidate together with the query (not separately) and scores how relevant it truly is, then reorders them. Reading them as a pair is what makes it sharper than the first pass.
  • Keep only the top few for the prompt. It is slower per item, so you run it just on the shortlist, never the whole index. Qwen3 ships a reranker too.
Slide 33

Query to grounded answer, end to end

  • Put it all together, end to end. A query gets an instruction and is embedded, then hybrid search pulls candidates by meaning and by keyword.
  • The reranker sharpens the order; the top chunks plus their citations go into the prompt; the LLM generates a grounded answer.
  • This is the payoff diagram — every piece we built, in one flow.
Slide 34

Measure retrieval, don't vibe it

  • You must measure retrieval, not vibe-check it. Three metrics. Context recall: did we fetch the chunks that contain the answer?
  • Context precision: of what we fetched, how much is actually relevant? Faithfulness: is the final answer grounded in those chunks, or did the model drift?
  • Build a small golden set of real questions with known answers, and tune chunking, dimensions and top-k against it. Measure, then change one thing at a time.

Slide 37

Three things to remember

  • Replay the spine. Index: chunk, embed, store. Retrieve: embed the query, hybrid search, rerank, generate with citations.
  • Safe defaults: recursive chunks around 256 to 512 tokens with 10 to 20 percent overlap, contextual retrieval, hybrid plus rerank, Qwen3-Embedding-8B in one consistent space, and always keep citations.
  • Three takeaways: chunking is the highest-leverage knob, hybrid beats pure vectors, and you must always measure.
