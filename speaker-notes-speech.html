Slide 1

Speech Technologies STT & TTS

  • Welcome to Day 2, Session 2. Session 1 turned sound into spectrograms and features and used Whisper to transcribe. Today we open the box: how speech-to-text really works, and the reverse direction, text-to-speech.
  • 90 minutes, intuition-first: no signal-processing maths. One animated demo. Banking voice examples throughout, and an open-source, self-hostable stance — sensitive customer audio cannot leave the bank.
  • Promise: by the end you can reason about an STT and a TTS system end to end, and explain how to adapt them to our languages and accents.
Slide 2

The frame

  • The whole session in one line. Reading aloud and listening back are mirror images of the same problem.
  • Text-to-speech goes text to sound; speech-to-text goes sound to text. Same pipeline, two directions.
  • Hold this image — we will literally build it as a mirror on the next slides, and call it back at the close.
Slide 3

From spectrograms to speech systems

  • Recap Session 1 in one breath. We learned that a waveform becomes a spectrogram, then Mel and MFCC features, and that Whisper can transcribe a call.
  • Today we go deeper: how that transcription actually happens inside the model, and the reverse — turning text back into natural speech.
  • We build directly on Session 1 — log-Mel spectrograms reappear as the input to STT and the output of the TTS acoustic model.
Slide 4

Two halves of one voice system

  • The core mental model: a voice system is a round-trip. Speech-to-text listens — audio becomes text. Text-to-speech speaks — text becomes audio.
  • An interactive voice response (IVR) line or a voice-bot needs both halves: it has to hear the caller and answer out loud.
  • Everything today slots into one side of this mirror or the other. Keep the round-trip in your head.
Slide 5

Siri, Alexa & Google Assistant are STT + TTS

  • You already use this every day. Siri, Alexa and Google Assistant are all the same STT-plus-TTS loop we just drew: a wake word, speech-to-text to understand you, a brain that decides the answer, then text-to-speech to reply out loud.
  • Apple Siri keeps a lot of the work on the iPhone itself; Amazon Alexa does most of it in the cloud and powers millions of Echo speakers; Google Assistant is strong across many languages.
  • The point for students: a bank voice-bot is the exact same loop. The only big difference is OCBC self-hosts every piece, so customer audio never leaves the bank.
Slide 6

Listening and speaking, mapped to the bank

  • Map the use cases. On the listening side: transcribe and analyse calls, voice commands, and compliance quality assurance over recorded conversations.
  • On the speaking side: voice-bot and IVR replies, accessibility for visually-impaired customers, and proactive voice alerts.
  • Almost every voice product a bank ships uses one or both directions. This is why both halves matter.
Slide 7

Sensitive voice never leaves the bank

  • The constraint that shapes every recommendation today. Sensitive customer voice cannot be sent to closed cloud speech APIs — that is audio leaving the bank.
  • We will explain how the big closed services work, but only for understanding. Everything we recommend is open-source and self-hostable.
  • Voice is biometric and deeply personal data. The rule is simple: audio stays inside OCBC infrastructure.
Slide 8

Part 1 — Speech-to-Text — Teaching Machines to Listen

  • Part 1, about 27 minutes. We define the task and its metric, then the heart of the session: the three architecture families for decoding speech.
  • Intuition over maths — no connectionist-temporal-classification equations, just pictures of how each family works.
  • End on the open-source landscape and a decision aid for choosing a model.
Slide 9

Word Error Rate (WER) is the yardstick

  • The task: audio in, text out. We score quality with Word Error Rate, WER: substitutions plus insertions plus deletions, divided by the number of reference words. Lower is better.
  • Worked example: the reference is six words; the model substituted one, inserted one, and deleted one — three errors over six words is fifty percent WER.
  • WER governs every model choice in this section. When we say one model is more accurate, we mean lower WER on the audio we care about.
Slide 10

Old automatic speech recognition (ASR): three glued parts

  • For context, the classic pipeline. Old automatic-speech-recognition systems stitched together three separate parts: an acoustic model, a pronunciation lexicon, and a language model — Kaldi is the classic toolkit.
  • Each part was trained and tuned separately, then glued. Powerful in expert hands, but complex and brittle.
  • This complexity is exactly why the field moved to end-to-end neural models, next.
Slide 11

One neural network, audio straight to text

  • The modern shift: one neural network learns audio-to-text directly, replacing the three glued parts. Simpler to train, and far more accurate given data.
  • The input is the log-Mel spectrogram from Session 1 — the picture of the sound. The network maps that picture straight to text.
  • Everything in the rest of this section is a different way of doing this one end-to-end mapping.
Slide 12

CTC vs. attention vs. transducer

  • The key teaching slide. Three architecture families decode speech, and the differences matter for the bank.
  • Connectionist temporal classification, CTC, emits a token per audio frame with a blank trick; it is fast and non-autoregressive but has no built-in language modelling. Attention encoder-decoder generates text token by token while attending to the audio — this is Whisper — accurate with built-in language modelling.
  • The transducer, such as recurrent-neural-network transducer (RNN-T), emits text as audio arrives — streaming-friendly, the choice for real-time voice-bots. We unpack each next.
Slide 13

Vote per frame, then collapse

  • CTC, intuitively. Picture each slice of audio voting on a letter. The same sound spans several frames, so you get repeats, and silence votes blank.
  • The trick: collapse repeated letters, then drop the blanks. H, H, blank, E, L, L, blank, L, O collapses to hello.
  • This is why CTC is fast — every frame is decoded at once, in parallel — but it has no sense of language, so it can misread words that sound alike.
Slide 14

Write text while looking back at the audio

  • Attention encoder-decoder, intuitively — a callback to the Day 1 transformer. The encoder reads the whole clip into a representation.
  • The decoder then writes the transcript one token at a time, and for each token it looks back at, or cross-attends to, the relevant part of the audio — the bright cells in this attention map.
  • Because it writes left to right using its own previous words, it has a built-in sense of language and is very accurate — but it is autoregressive, so slower, and it can occasionally hallucinate words that were never said.
Slide 15

Emit words as you speak

  • The transducer, intuitively. Instead of waiting for the whole clip, it emits words as you speak.
  • As each chunk of audio arrives, the model decides whether it has heard enough to output the next word — so text streams out in near real-time.
  • That low latency is exactly what a live voice-bot needs: the caller should not wait for silence before the system reacts.
Slide 16

Open-source STT models you can use

  • The open-source speech-to-text models you can use, mapped to the three families. Whisper, including large-v3-turbo, is the attention encoder-decoder — multilingual with a huge ecosystem, and the most popular open option.
  • wav2vec2 and XLS-R are CTC, self-supervised, and excellent bases for fine-tuning low-resource languages. NVIDIA Parakeet and Canary are transducer or encoder-decoder, optimised for speed or accuracy. Kaldi remains the classic modular toolkit.
  • All open-source and free to run on your own machine. Model names move fast — present these as of 2026 and verify the current best before committing.
Slide 17

Model + toolchain = production STT

  • Production STT is a model plus a toolchain. Around Whisper sits a practical open-source ecosystem.
  • faster-whisper speeds inference; WhisperX adds word-level timestamps, alignment and voice-activity detection; whisper.cpp runs on central-processing-unit or on-device; Silero VAD finds where speech actually is.
  • The point: do not ship the raw model alone. Real systems combine the model with these tools.
Slide 18

What real call analytics adds on top

  • Call analytics needs more than the words. Diarization labels who spoke when — agent versus customer. Voice-activity detection separates speech from silence and hold music.
  • Punctuation and casing make a transcript readable; timestamps let you jump to the exact moment a phrase was said, which compliance needs.
  • Open-source options exist for each of these. Transcription is the start, not the whole job.
Slide 19

Match the model to the job

  • A decision aid for choosing an STT model. Weigh accuracy, latency, languages and hardware.
  • Overnight compliance batch? Accuracy first — Whisper large. Live voice-bot? Streaming transducer for low latency. Mixed Indonesian-English calls? Whisper, for its multilingual strength.
  • There is no single best model — match the model to the job.
Slide 20

Part 2 — Text-to-Speech — Teaching Machines to Speak

  • Part 2, about 25 minutes. Now the mirror image — turning text into natural-sounding audio.
  • We assemble the three-stage pipeline, then trace how acoustic models evolved from sequential to parallel to end-to-end.
  • Same open-source, self-hostable stance throughout.
Slide 21

Text in, natural speech out

  • The TTS task: text to natural-sounding audio. We judge it on naturalness — often a one-to-five human rating called Mean Opinion Score, MOS — plus intelligibility and speed.
  • It is harder than it looks. The same sentence has infinitely many valid readings: where you pause, stress, and raise pitch all change with intent.
  • So TTS is not a lookup — the model must decide a plausible prosody every time.
Slide 22

Text → frontend → acoustic model → vocoder

  • The TTS spine, three stages. First the text frontend normalises the input — dollar-1,250 becomes one thousand two hundred fifty — and grapheme-to-phoneme, G2P, turns letters into sound units.
  • Second, the acoustic model turns those phonemes into a Mel spectrogram — the picture of the sound from Session 1, now with decided rhythm and pitch.
  • Third, the vocoder turns that spectrogram into an actual waveform you can hear. Text, to phonemes, to spectrogram, to wave.
Slide 23

Normalize the text, then map letters to sounds

  • Stage one: the unglamorous but critical text frontend. Normalization expands numbers, dates, currency and abbreviations into spoken words.
  • Then grapheme-to-phoneme maps letters to sound units — how c-a-t becomes the phonemes K AE T. This is deeply language-dependent.
  • Flag it now: this stage is where language adaptation begins, because every language pronounces letters differently.
Slide 24

Phonemes become the spectrogram picture

  • Stage two: the acoustic model predicts the Mel spectrogram from the phonemes — the same picture we met in Session 1, but now generated rather than measured.
  • This is where prosody lives: rhythm, stress and pitch are all decided here, frame by frame.
  • Get this stage wrong and speech sounds robotic or mis-stressed, even if every word is correct.
Slide 25

Turn the picture into sound

  • Stage three: the vocoder turns the spectrogram into an audible waveform. This is the final mile, and its quality makes or breaks naturalness.
  • A modern open-source vocoder like HiFi-GAN produces clean, natural audio fast.
  • A great acoustic model with a poor vocoder still sounds buzzy — naturalness lives in both stages two and three.
Slide 26

Sequential → parallel → end-to-end

  • How acoustic models matured. Tacotron 2 is an attention sequence-to-sequence model that generates frames one after another — natural, but slow, and it can skip or repeat words.
  • FastSpeech 2 predicts durations up front and generates the whole spectrogram in parallel — fast and stable.
  • VITS and other end-to-end models fuse the acoustic model and vocoder into one network: text straight to wave. The trend is sequential, to parallel, to one unified model.
Slide 27

Mimic a voice from seconds of audio

  • Newer open models can clone a voice from seconds of audio — zero-shot TTS, including LLM-style speech models like XTTS v2.
  • That is powerful for a consistent branded voice across every IVR prompt. But for a bank it carries real risk.
  • Cloning is a deepfake and voice-biometrics threat. Flag consent and misuse explicitly — capability here outruns comfort, and governance must keep up.
Slide 28

Open-source TTS models you can use

  • Self-hostable TTS options. Piper is fast and lightweight, runs on-device, and is ideal for IVR prompts. Coqui TTS and XTTS are multilingual with voice cloning.
  • ESPnet and PaddleSpeech are research toolkits with many recipes; VITS is the end-to-end option.
  • All open-source. As with STT, names move fast — present as of 2026 and verify.
Slide 29

Match the voice to the use case

  • Choosing a TTS model: weigh naturalness, latency, footprint, and languages or voices.
  • On-device IVR prompts that must be instant and tiny? Piper. An expressive, multilingual voice-bot? XTTS or VITS.
  • Again, no single winner — pick by the constraint that dominates the use case.
Slide 30

Data residency decides it

  • An honest comparison. Closed cloud TTS and STT can be a touch more turnkey and occasionally a notch more natural.
  • But they send audio off-premise — disqualifying for sensitive banking voice. That single fact, data residency, decides it.
  • Open models have closed the gap and keep every byte of audio inside OCBC. Frame it as a deliberate, well-understood trade-off, not a compromise.
Slide 31

Part 3 — Making It Work for Our Languages & Voices

  • Part 3, about 18 minutes. Base models stumble on our accents, our code-switching, and our product names.
  • We climb the adaptation ladder from cheap prompting to full fine-tuning, with LoRA as the practical default.
  • The concrete case throughout: Indonesian-English code-switched banking calls.
Slide 32

Base models stumble on our audio

  • Why adaptation is needed. Base models drop accuracy on accents, on code-switching between Indonesian and English mid-sentence, on local terms, on product names, and on noisy phone audio.
  • Banking piles on jargon and proper nouns the model never saw in training.
  • This is the Indonesian and Bahasa reality for our audience — the gap is real and measurable in WER.
Slide 33

Cheap → expensive: climb only as needed

  • The adaptation ladder, cheapest to most expensive. Rung one: prompting, biasing and a custom vocabulary — bias the model toward product names and jargon. Almost free.
  • Rung two: a better frontend or lexicon — fix G2P pronunciations and add domain terms, mainly for TTS. Rung three: parameter-efficient fine-tuning with LoRA or adapters — small added weights, little data, avoids forgetting.
  • Rung four: full fine-tuning — retrain on labelled domain and accent audio. Most powerful, most data and compute. Climb only as high as the gap demands.
Slide 34

Nudge a base model toward our audio

  • Fine-tuning, intuitively. Start from a multilingual base such as Whisper or XLS-R, then continue training on your own audio.
  • You do not start from scratch — you nudge a model that already knows speech toward your accent and vocabulary.
  • Even ten minutes to twenty hours of in-domain data can sharply cut WER on a specific accent. A little targeted data goes a long way.
Slide 35

Freeze the base, train a tiny adapter

  • LoRA and adapters — the practical default, and a callback to Day 1 fine-tuning. Freeze the big base model and train only tiny inserted matrices.
  • That is cheap, fast, and swappable: you can keep a separate small adapter per accent and load the right one on demand.
  • Because the base stays frozen, you avoid catastrophic forgetting — the model keeps its general ability while gaining your specialism.
Slide 36

Adaptation needs labelled audio

  • Data is the real bottleneck. Adaptation needs labelled audio — transcribed calls — and that is the hard part to get.
  • Tactics: transcribe a sample of in-house calls, use public Indonesian corpora like Common Voice, and use TTS data augmentation — synthesise accented audio to bootstrap STT training.
  • Flag privacy and consent before using any real customer calls. Data sourcing is a governance question as much as a technical one.
Slide 37

Measure on our data, not a leaderboard

  • Evaluate adaptation properly. Measure WER on a held-out set of our own calls and accents — not a generic leaderboard.
  • Track error per accent and per term, so you know whether product names specifically improved. For TTS, run MOS listening tests with real listeners.
  • Do not trust leaderboard numbers for your audio. The only score that matters is on data that looks like production.
Slide 38

Capability is not permission

  • Governance for voice — bank-specific guardrails. Get explicit consent for voice biometrics, and treat cloning as a deepfake risk to control.
  • Transcripts contain personally identifiable information, so apply retention limits and access controls. Keep a human in the loop for any compliance decision.
  • Capability is not permission. Responsible-use framing is non-negotiable for customer voice.

Part 4 — Speech Tech at OCBC

  • The last stretch, about 8 minutes. We tie both directions into one end-to-end voice-bot.
  • Everything open-source and on-premise — audio never leaves OCBC.

Slide 40

Listen → understand → speak, on-prem

  • Tie the day together: an end-to-end voice-bot, entirely open-source and on-premise. The caller speaks; voice-activity detection plus streaming STT — a transducer or Whisper — turns it into text.
  • That text goes to the Day-1 RAG and LLM stack, which picks an answer. TTS — Piper or XTTS — speaks the answer back.
  • Every block is self-hosted; audio never leaves OCBC. Specifics here are illustrative unless we have real internal details.

Slide 42

Listen, and speak — on our terms

  • Land the plane. The mirror: STT listens, TTS speaks — two directions of one pipeline.
  • The mechanisms: STT decodes via CTC, attention, or transducer; TTS runs frontend, then acoustic model, then vocoder. Adaptation climbs from prompting to frontend to LoRA to full fine-tuning — always open-source.
  • Three takeaways: pick the STT architecture by latency versus accuracy; naturalness lives in the acoustic model plus vocoder; adapt to our languages and measure on our data. Pointers: the Whisper repo, Coqui and Piper, and the Hugging Face Open ASR leaderboard. Questions.
